## System Overview

The Algorithmic Trading & Market Surveillance System (ATMSS) is the enterprise platform responsible for executing, monitoring, and auditing all proprietary and client-directed equity, options, and fixed income trades across the organization's trading operations. The system processes an average of 4.2 million trade events per day and maintains a complete, immutable audit trail for regulatory examination purposes.

A primary function of the ATMSS is market surveillance — the continuous monitoring of all trading activity for patterns indicative of market manipulation, insider trading, wash trading, and other forms of securities fraud. Market surveillance is a legal obligation under securities law and is enforced by the Securities and Exchange Commission (SEC) and the Financial Industry Regulatory Authority (FINRA). Failure to detect and report suspicious trading activity exposes the organization to significant regulatory liability, including suspension of trading privileges, fines, and criminal referral.

The ATMSS's surveillance capabilities include real-time pattern detection, post-trade analytics, cross-market surveillance, and automated regulatory reporting. The system integrates with FINRA's Order Audit Trail System (OATS) and the Consolidated Audit Trail (CAT) to provide regulators with complete visibility into trading activity.

## Trading Architecture

### Core System Components

**1. Order Management System (OMS)**
The OMS manages the full lifecycle of every trade order from initial receipt to execution confirmation. It enforces pre-trade risk controls, routes orders to appropriate execution venues (NYSE, NASDAQ, CBOE, dark pools), and maintains the authoritative order book. All order events — including placements, modifications, cancellations, and executions — are logged in real time with microsecond-precision timestamps.

**2. Execution Management System (EMS)**
The EMS handles smart order routing and algorithmic execution strategies. It interfaces with exchange matching engines via co-located direct market access connections and manages order slicing for large institutional trades to minimize market impact. The EMS records all execution reports for post-trade analysis and surveillance.

**3. Market Surveillance Engine (MSE)**
The MSE is the real-time monitoring component responsible for fraud detection and market manipulation surveillance. It processes the full trade event stream and applies rule-based detection algorithms, statistical anomaly detection, and machine learning models to identify suspicious trading patterns. Alerts generated by the MSE are routed to the compliance team for investigation.

**4. Risk Management Platform (RMP)**
The RMP enforces real-time pre-trade and post-trade risk limits including position limits, sector concentration limits, value-at-risk (VaR) thresholds, and counterparty credit limits. Trades that would breach risk limits are blocked at the OMS level before reaching the execution layer.

**5. Regulatory Reporting Engine (RRE)**
The RRE automates the generation and submission of all required regulatory reports including FINRA OATS/CAT reports, SEC Large Trader reports, Form PF filings, and Blue Sheet requests from regulators in response to investigations.

**6. Trade Surveillance Data Warehouse**
A dedicated time-series data warehouse retaining the complete history of all trade events, order book states, surveillance alerts, and investigation outcomes. Data is retained for a minimum of 7 years in accordance with SEC Rule 17a-4. The warehouse supports forensic investigation by compliance and legal teams.

## Trade Data Schema

The primary trade dataset (`stock_trades.csv`) conforms to the following schema. Each row represents one executed trade event.

| Column | Data Type | Description |
|---|---|---|
| `trade_id` | String | Unique trade identifier. Format: `TRD` followed by a 5-digit zero-padded integer (e.g., `TRD00001`). Assigned at time of execution. Immutable. |
| `trader_id` | String | Identifier for the individual trader or algorithmic strategy that submitted the order. Format: `TR` followed by a 3-digit number. Links to the Trader Profile in the OMS. |
| `ticker` | String | Exchange ticker symbol of the security traded (e.g., `AAPL`, `MSFT`, `NVDA`). |
| `trade_type` | String | Classification of the trade instruction. Valid values: `Buy`, `Sell`, `Short Sell`, `Options Call`, `Options Put`. |
| `shares` | Integer | Number of shares (for equity trades) or contracts (for options trades) executed. |
| `price_per_share` | Float | Execution price per share in US Dollars at the time of trade completion. |
| `total_value_usd` | Float | Total dollar value of the trade. Calculated as `shares × price_per_share`. For options, calculated as `contracts × price × 100`. |
| `exchange` | String | The exchange or venue on which the trade was executed. Valid values: `NYSE`, `NASDAQ`, `CBOE`. |
| `timestamp` | DateTime | Timestamp of trade execution in Eastern Time (ET). Format: `YYYY-MM-DD HH:MM:SS`. Standard market hours are 09:30–16:00 ET. |
| `sector` | String | GICS sector classification of the traded security. Values include: `Technology`, `Finance`, `Healthcare`, `Energy`, `Retail`, `Consumer`. |
| `is_flagged` | Boolean | Indicates whether the trade has been flagged by the Market Surveillance Engine for potential misconduct or anomalous behavior. Values: `TRUE` or `FALSE`. Flagged trades require compliance review. |

## Market Surveillance Rules

The Market Surveillance Engine applies the following surveillance rules continuously to the trade event stream. Each rule is designed to detect specific categories of fraud, manipulation, or regulatory violation.

### Active Surveillance Rules

| Rule ID | Rule Name | Trigger | Severity | Regulatory Basis |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| MS-001 | Unusual Volume Pre-Event | Trader accumulates >3x normal position in a security within 5 days of a material corporate event | Critical | SEC Rule 10b-5 |
| MS-002 | After-Hours Large Trade | Trade with total value >$500,000 executed outside market hours (before 09:30 or after 16:00 ET) | High | FINRA Rule 2010 |
| MS-003 | Cross-Trade Detection | Buy and sell of the same security between two trader IDs in the same firm at the same price within 60 seconds | Critical | SEC Rule 10b-5 / Wash Trading |
| MS-004 | Layering Detection | Submission of large orders that are cancelled within 500ms at a rate >80% of all orders submitted | Critical | Dodd-Frank Section 747 |
| MS-005 | Momentum Ignition | Pattern of small aggressive orders designed to trigger stop-loss orders in other participants, followed by a position reversal | High | SEC Regulation NMS |
| MS-006 | Spoofing Detection | Repeated pattern of large order placement and cancellation immediately prior to smaller orders on the opposite side | Critical | Dodd-Frank Section 747 |
| MS-007 | Coordinated Trading | Multiple trader IDs exhibiting correlated order timing and size patterns suggesting coordination | High | SEC Rule 10b-5 |
| MS-008 | Short Sell Threshold | Short sale position exceeding 5% of average daily volume in a single session | Medium | SEC Regulation SHO |
| MS-009 | Options Anomaly Pre-Event | Unusual options volume (>5x 30-day average) in calls or puts of a security within 10 days of a material event | Critical | SEC Rule 10b-5 |
| MS-010 | Large Trader Concentration | Single trader accumulates a position representing >15% of a security's daily traded volume | High | SEC Large Trader Rule |

## Suspicious Trading Pattern Detection

### Insider Trading Indicators

Insider trading is the buying or selling of securities based on material, non-public information (MNPI) in violation of a duty to keep that information confidential. It is a federal crime under Section 10(b) of the Securities Exchange Act of 1934 and SEC Rule 10b-5. The ATMSS surveillance engine uses the following indicators to detect potential insider trading:

**Pre-Event Accumulation:** A trader or closely connected group of traders accumulates an unusually large position in a security shortly before the public announcement of a material corporate event (earnings surprise, M&A announcement, regulatory approval, executive change). The concentration and timing of the position relative to the event are the primary fraud indicators.

**Abnormal Options Activity:** Unusual options volume — particularly out-of-the-money calls (anticipating price increase) or puts (anticipating price decline) — purchased shortly before a material announcement. Options amplify gains dramatically when the underlying event is known in advance, making them the instrument of choice for insider trading fraud.

**Profit Concentration:** A single trader realizing outsized, consistent profits on trades that are concentrated in specific securities and time windows correlated with public announcements. Statistical analysis compares the trader's win rate and profit factor against expected distributions for their strategy and market conditions.

**Communication Correlation:** Where surveillance data can be correlated with communication records, patterns of trading that closely follow known information flows (board meetings, analyst calls, clinical trial results) are a strong indicator of insider trading fraud.

The ATMSS flags potential insider trading events for referral to the compliance team, which coordinates with legal counsel to determine whether a voluntary report to the SEC Division of Enforcement is warranted.

### Market Manipulation Patterns

Market manipulation involves intentional actions designed to create an artificial price or create a false impression of active trading in a security. It is prohibited under Section 9 and Section 10(b) of the Securities Exchange Act.

**Pump and Dump:**
A coordinated scheme in which a group of traders acquires a significant position in a security, then engages in misleading promotional activity or coordinated aggressive buying to artificially inflate the price (the "pump"), attracting other investors. The manipulators then sell their positions at the inflated price (the "dump"), leaving other investors with losses. Pump and dump schemes are one of the oldest forms of securities fraud and are particularly prevalent in low-liquidity or small-capitalization securities.

Surveillance indicators: Correlated buy orders from multiple trader IDs in the same security within a compressed time window; price movement significantly exceeding sector and market benchmarks; followed by concentrated selling by the same group.

**Spoofing:**
The placement of large orders with no intent to execute, designed to create a false impression of supply or demand and move the price artificially. The orders are cancelled before execution. Spoofing was explicitly criminalized in the United States by the Dodd-Frank Act (2010) and has resulted in significant criminal convictions and civil fraud penalties.

Surveillance indicators: High order-to-trade ratio; order cancellations within milliseconds of placement; correlation between large order placements and price movement; resumption of the original order flow after the target price is reached.

**Momentum Ignition:**
Placing a series of orders designed to trigger other participants' algorithmic stop-loss or momentum-following systems, creating a temporary price movement. The manipulator then reverses their position to profit from the artificial movement they created.

### Wash Trading Detection

Wash trading involves the simultaneous or near-simultaneous purchase and sale of the same security by the same beneficial owner or by coordinated accounts, creating the appearance of active trading without any genuine change in ownership or economic exposure. Wash trading is prohibited under Section 9(a)(1) of the Securities Exchange Act as a form of market fraud, because it artificially inflates reported trading volume and may be used to generate false transaction records for tax fraud or to make an illiquid security appear liquid.

**Detection Methodology:**

The MSE identifies wash trading by analyzing:
1. Buy and sell orders in the same security by the same trader ID within a defined time window (default: 60 minutes)
2. Offsetting trades between multiple trader IDs that share identifiable connections (same firm, same IP range, same beneficial owner based on KYC records)
3. Circular trading patterns involving three or more parties where the net economic position is unchanged after the sequence completes
4. Statistical anomaly detection on individual trader profit-and-loss distributions; wash trading produces an unusually flat P&L profile with high transaction volume

When wash trading is suspected, the case is escalated immediately to compliance and legal. Confirmed wash trading is reported to FINRA and the SEC as required by FINRA Rule 2010 and constitutes securities fraud.

## Regulatory Compliance (SEC, FINRA)

### Securities and Exchange Commission (SEC)

The ATMSS is designed to comply with all applicable SEC regulations, including:

- **Securities Exchange Act of 1934, Section 10(b) and Rule 10b-5:** Prohibits fraud and manipulation in connection with the purchase or sale of securities
- **Regulation NMS (National Market System):** Requires best execution and fair access to market data
- **SEC Rule 17a-4:** Records retention requirement; all trade records must be retained for 7 years in WORM (write-once-read-many) format
- **SEC Large Trader Rule (Rule 13h-1):** Requires large traders (those exceeding $20 million in a day or $200 million in a month) to register with the SEC and comply with enhanced recordkeeping
- **Dodd-Frank Act, Section 929X:** Establishes whistleblower protections and incentives for reporting securities fraud to the SEC

### FINRA (Financial Industry Regulatory Authority)

- **FINRA Rule 3110:** Supervision. Requires firms to establish supervisory systems and procedures to detect and prevent fraud, manipulation, and regulatory violations
- **FINRA Rule 4511:** General records requirements, including trade records
- **FINRA OATS / CAT Reporting:** All orders must be reported to the Consolidated Audit Trail (CAT) in real time for regulatory surveillance purposes
- **FINRA Rule 2010:** Standards of commercial honor. Prohibits all forms of fraud, manipulation, and deceptive practices
- **FINRA Rule 4370:** Business Continuity Planning, including systems used for fraud detection and surveillance

## Risk Management Framework

### Pre-Trade Risk Controls

Pre-trade risk controls are enforced at the OMS level before any order is submitted to an exchange. These controls prevent risk limit breaches and order errors that could cause market disruption.

| Control | Limit | Action |
|---|---|---|
| Single Order Size | Maximum $10M notional per order | Auto-reject with alert |
| Daily Loss Limit | Maximum 2% of firm AUM | Trading halt; risk manager notification |
| Position Concentration | Maximum 15% of security's ADV | Order held; risk manager approval required |
| VaR Limit | 99% 1-day VaR not to exceed approved limit | Trading halt for breaching desk |
| Sector Concentration | Maximum 25% portfolio allocation per sector | Warning at 20%; block at 25% |
| Short Sell Locate | Short sell requires confirmed locate | Auto-reject if locate not confirmed |

### Post-Trade Risk Monitoring

Post-trade risk monitoring runs continuously on the full portfolio and generates alerts for:

- Mark-to-market losses exceeding intraday loss limits
- Concentration drifts caused by market movements
- Counterparty credit exposure approaching limits
- Unmatched or failed trade settlements

## Alert and Escalation Procedures

### Surveillance Alert Levels

| Alert Level | Description | Response Time | Escalation Path |
|---|---|---|---|
| L1 — Low | Statistical anomaly; low probability of fraud | Next business day | Surveillance analyst review |
| L2 — Moderate | Pattern consistent with known manipulation technique; investigation warranted | 24 hours | Senior compliance officer |
| L3 — High | Strong indicators of fraud or regulatory violation; credible case for enforcement | 4 hours | Chief Compliance Officer (CCO) + Legal |
| L4 — Critical | Clear evidence of insider trading, fraud, or manipulation in progress | Immediate | CCO + Legal + Executive Committee; SEC/FINRA referral evaluated |

### Regulatory Referral Process

When the compliance team determines that suspicious trading activity constitutes a credible violation of securities law, the following referral process is initiated:

1. **Internal Documentation:** Full case file assembled including trade records, surveillance alert details, trader communications (if available), and compliance analysis
2. **Legal Review:** In-house and outside counsel review the case for legal sufficiency
3. **Voluntary Disclosure Evaluation:** Determination of whether voluntary disclosure to SEC or FINRA is required or advisable
4. **Regulator Notification:** If warranted, notification submitted to SEC Division of Enforcement and/or FINRA Market Regulation
5. **Cooperation:** Full cooperation with any subsequent regulatory investigation, including Blue Sheet production and witness availability
6. **Internal Remediation:** Regardless of external referral, internal disciplinary action taken against responsible individuals; system controls updated to prevent recurrence
